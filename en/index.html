ToDesk Latency Test + Bandwidth Calculator

Whether remote control feels smooth comes down to two things: network round-trip latency (RTT) and available bandwidth. Both tools below run locally in your browser — ① measure RTT and jitter to several public endpoints and rank remote-control smoothness; ② convert bandwidth units and estimate the resolution a given bandwidth can drive. Baselines come from Microsoft's official Remote Desktop docs and ToDesk's specs (see sources at the bottom).

Tool 1: network round-trip (Ping) test

Press the button and your browser sends a few requests to each public endpoint and times the round trip. We take the best RTT per endpoint (closest to the real network round trip) and rate remote-control smoothness using the latency tiers below.

Note: this measures the network round trip to public endpoints — a proxy for the network leg of remote control. Real remote-control latency also adds capture, encode/decode and display time. Results depend on your network and endpoint distance.

Tool 2: bandwidth converter + quality estimate

Enter a bandwidth value, pick a unit, and it converts to common units and — using Microsoft's official Remote Desktop recommendations — estimates roughly what resolution this bandwidth can drive at 30fps.

How to read remote-control latency (tiers)

Interactive remote control is more sensitive to latency than to bandwidth. Microsoft notes that highly interactive / assistive scenarios want RTT ≤20ms for the best experience. The table below combines that anchor with industry experience — find your tier.

Frequently asked questions

What latency counts as normal for remote control?
As a rule of thumb: RTT under 50ms feels near-instant, under 100ms is the recommended range, 100–150ms is usable but you feel the lag, over 150ms is clearly choppy, and 200ms or more is nearly unusable. Microsoft also notes that highly interactive/assistive scenarios want RTT ≤20ms for the best experience.
Roughly how much bandwidth does ToDesk remote control need?
Per Microsoft's official Remote Desktop recommendation (30fps): about 3 Mbps for 720p, 5 Mbps for 1080p and 15 Mbps for 4K. Modern remote control uses adaptive encoding, so real needs are often lower.
Which matters more, latency or bandwidth?
For interactive remote control, latency matters more: even with enough bandwidth, high latency/jitter still feels laggy. Microsoft's dynamic bitrate actually lowers quality when RTT is high to keep things smooth.
What does this latency test actually measure?
Your browser measures the network round trip to public endpoints (Cloudflare, Google, etc.) — a proxy for the network leg of remote control. Real remote-control latency also adds capture, encode/decode and display time.
Why is it still laggy when my bandwidth is high?
Usually it's latency, jitter or packet loss, not bandwidth. Remote control is sensitive to RTT and stability; high jitter hurts more than a high average.
What's the highest quality ToDesk can reach?
Its official site claims up to 8K, 360 FPS and 4:4:4 true-color lossless (AV1 codec / ZeroSync engine), given the right plan and enough bandwidth.
How much bandwidth does 1080p video take over remote control?
Microsoft's example: a half-screen 30fps video is about 8.5–9.5 Mbps by default, but only about 2.5–2.8 Mbps with H.264/AVC-444 — the codec makes a big difference.
How do I convert Mbps and MB/s?
1 MB/s = 8 Mbps, and 1 Mbps = 0.125 MB/s. The bandwidth converter on this page does it for all common units automatically.

Quick real-world triage

  • Typing and dragging windows lags but is usable: RTT is usually 100–150ms; a nearer endpoint or a wired connection often pulls it back under 100ms.
  • Picture is blurry and only sharpens after you stop moving: limited bandwidth triggered adaptive downscaling; check the bandwidth converter against the quality tiers and lower the target resolution if needed.
  • RTT isn't high but it comes and goes: focus on jitter — high jitter hurts more than a high average; check Wi-Fi interference, switch to 5GHz or wired.
  • Cross-border remote control is very laggy: ordinary public routing detours, so RTT and packet loss are both high; a nearer relay or the product's global nodes help a lot.
  • 4K remote control has enough bandwidth but drops frames: high res/frame rate also taxes encode/decode compute, not just bandwidth; try 1080p or a lower frame rate.
